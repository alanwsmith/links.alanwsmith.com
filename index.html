or: How to grow your digital garden with sub-domains

First things first: I'm on Twitter a lot, Twitch occasionally, and will one day return to Instagram. I'm also playing with a podcast.

~

Now, before we get to the rest of the links, we need to tell a story about digital gardens.

I first heard about digital gardens from some fine folks in the Party Corgi Network. They lead me to these posts by Maggie Appleton and Joel Hooks. To quote Maggie Appleton's description:

Rather than presenting a set of polished articles, displayed in reverse chronological order, these sites act more like free form, work-in-progress wikis.

That idea stuck a chord.

I haven't been so excited about playing on the web since the '90s. I switched my site from software designed to make a blog to a framework with more freedom to move. I've been experimenting with the layout, design, and content ever since. But, I realized I was feeling resistance.

Whenever I wanted to play with an idea, I'd have to take into account the existing setup and structure. A simple example is the width of pages. Right now, everything's the same on my site. Defined in a single configuration template. To make something a different width, I'd have to rewire the site for multiple templates, then make them.

While I've got enought experience that that wouldn't be particullarly hard, it would be work. And that work is friction. And that friction stops me. Or, more accurately, keeps me from getting started.

When I want to play with an idea, I want to play with it. Not work on the infrastructue that supports it.

~

Enter Dusty Domains

Dusty Domains was a charity project (and genius marketing play) from Netlify at the end of 2021. The idea was to take unused domains and actually publish something on them by the end of the year. Each one published added $500 to a pool for coding related charaties.

That sounded great to me. So, I built JackTorrance.blog. I turned it around in three days and had an absolute blast doing it. I started with nothing more than the domain and an idea and turned it into something I'm really proud of.

Something also clicked during the build. I realized I hadn't experienced any of that getting started friction I'd been running into with ideas for my site. The empty domain was a clean slate. No worrying about the width of pages. I could make them whatever size I wanted without hassle.

And, it went way byond that. The software for my main site had no baring on what I used for the new domain. I could use the same framework, a completley different one, or roll back to using vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

If you had said the words "domains are independent" to me, I would have responded, "umm, yes. and?" But, the implications never fully registered. I didn't grok it.

Now, I do.

~

I have one domain: alanwsmith.com

Though, as with most setups, that means I've really got two domains: `alanwsmith.com` (without a third level `www`) and the `www.alanwsmith.com` version with it. I've gone back and forth over the years between serving my site off one or the other. But, for all intents and purposes, they're the same thing.

I've also split things out in the past when I needed to use another service. For example, serving site pages off `www.alanwsmith.com` and then using `i.alanwsmith.com` pointed to a CDN to serve images. Again, really just the same site.

And that's been my mental model. A cohesive domain that sometimes includes sub-domains for functionality.

~

But then, something happened.

Dusty Domains was getting towards the end of its run and they hadn't hit the total goal for donations via published sites. I'd seen one of the domains they had listed as inspiration called Hawk Dance. It's a single page site with some animation. I figured I could pull that off. So, I made hacking.alanwsmith.com

Boom. $500 for charity.

They were still short. So, I made AreYouCurrentlyAlive.alanwsmith.com

$500 more.

Both sites took about fifteen minutes to build and launch from the time I picked the idea. I was ready to do twenty more if they needed.

And that's when the bit flipped. I don't have one domain. I have infintie domains.

Each one, a clean slate.

~

Not gonna lie, this still blows my mind.

In the before times, the only time I'd branch off to a sub-domain is if I needed multiple services or servers to power a site. For example, running `www.example.com` with my software and then pointing `i.example.com` to a CDN to serve images.

In all thoses cases, the sub-domains were really part of the primary site. If the functionality could have been provided without the split, it would have.

That was my mental model. Everything under the second level domain was part of the same site. If I wanted a new site, I needed a new second level domain. A method that gets expensive fast.

And then the other bit flipped.

Except, that's not quite right. I have one second level domain: alanwsmith.com.

With that one, I have infinite. All branching from the first through the power of sub-domains.
